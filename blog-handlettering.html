Handlettering

A foray into the art of drawing letters

Recently, I am becoming more and more fascinated by the art and craft of handlettering. What is it? Well, I think of it this way: while typography is using existing, pre-designed letters and arranging them in a visually meaningful and pleasing way, lettering is doing the same but by hand, basically drawing each individual letter.

I greatly admire artists who can do this, such as Holly Dunn. She creates these gorgeous illustrated and hand-lettered covers like this one for "The Beholder". Covers like this make me want to buy the book just so that I can have such a beautiful thing on my bookshelf.

And to create a cover like this the designer has to have not only the expertise of typography but also, on top of it, the skill and dexterity to manually draw out each individual letter.

I’ve been working on a premade cover for a spy thriller set during World War II and I ended up spending a lot of time looking for the perfect font. During my research, I came across this cover.

The Swiss Spy has been published in 2015. Furthermore, Alex Gerlis has written several more books set in the same time period. Seeing as I enjoy a good spy story I will need to check them out.

But back to the subject of fonts. I believe the font used on the covers of these books is Globe SB Bold. It’s a beautiful type, with distinctly art deco proportions and yet still very simple. However, it requires a paid license if you want to use it on the cover of a book. So I searched some more and I found a bunch of google fonts that I think make for good alternatives. They have the similar deco style and, like all google fonts, are open source and free even for commercial use.
